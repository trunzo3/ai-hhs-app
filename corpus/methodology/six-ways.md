# Six Ways to Use AI in HHS Work

HHS managers face a constant flow of complex writing and thinking tasks. AI is most useful when you match the right approach to the right task type. The IQmeetEQ methodology organizes AI use into six task categories: Draft, Distill, Prepare, Synthesize, Critique, and Brainstorm.

## Draft

Draft tasks involve creating new written content from scratch or from raw material.

Examples: drafting emails to teams or leadership, writing policy summaries, preparing talking points, creating agendas, writing memos, drafting board presentations, composing cover letters for reports.

The key question: What is the audience, and what should they do or feel after reading this?

When to use Draft: When you need to produce written output and want a strong first draft to react to and refine, rather than starting from a blank page.

## Distill

Distill tasks involve extracting the essential meaning from complex or lengthy material.

Examples: breaking down an ACL or policy letter, summarizing a report, extracting action items from meeting notes, translating regulations into plain language, creating a one-page summary of a 40-page document.

The key question: What does someone need to know from this, and what can they ignore?

When to use Distill: When you have more information than you can act on, or when others need to understand something complex without reading the original.

## Prepare

Prepare tasks involve getting ready for a conversation, meeting, or situation.

Examples: preparing for a difficult performance conversation, planning a community meeting, anticipating objections before a budget presentation, thinking through how to approach a resistant supervisor, preparing talking points for a legislative briefing.

The key question: Who will you be talking to, what do you need to accomplish, and what obstacles might you face?

When to use Prepare: When you have an important interaction coming up and want to think it through with a thought partner who will challenge your assumptions and help you anticipate what you're not thinking about.

## Synthesize

Synthesize tasks involve combining insights from multiple sources into a coherent whole.

Examples: building a case for change that draws on data, best practices, and lived experience; creating a comprehensive view of a program from multiple reports; combining staff feedback from different sources into themes; developing a strategic recommendation from disparate evidence.

The key question: What pattern or conclusion emerges when you look at these things together?

When to use Synthesize: When you have multiple pieces of evidence, data, or perspectives and need to make sense of them as a whole.

## Critique

Critique tasks involve getting rigorous feedback on something you've already written or decided.

Examples: getting feedback on a draft memo, pressure-testing a proposal before presenting it, identifying weaknesses in a plan, checking a draft policy for gaps, stress-testing an argument.

The key question: What would someone who disagrees with this say, and is that critique valid?

When to use Critique: When you want honest feedback on your work before it goes to your audience. The best critique prompts ask the AI to identify specific weaknesses, not just confirm your thinking.

## Brainstorm

Brainstorm tasks involve generating options, ideas, or possibilities you haven't thought of yet.

Examples: generating solutions to a persistent problem, exploring approaches to improving staff retention, brainstorming ways to engage community members, developing options for a reorganization, thinking through approaches to a complex case situation.

The key question: What haven't I thought of yet?

When to use Brainstorm: When you're stuck, when you need more options than you currently have, or when you want to challenge your own thinking. The best brainstorms combine divergent generation (many ideas) with convergent filtering (which ones matter).
