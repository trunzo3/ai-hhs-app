# Red/Yellow/Green Data Safety Framework

Before using AI for any task that involves client information, case data, or protected information, HHS workers should run a quick Red/Yellow/Green assessment. The goal is not to stop people from using AI — it's to make sure they use it in ways that protect clients and comply with privacy requirements.

## The Three Categories

**Green — Low risk, proceed with confidence**

Green tasks involve information that is already public, doesn't identify individuals, or is general enough that privacy risk is minimal.

Examples:
- Summarizing a publicly available ACL or policy letter
- Drafting a general email to staff about a policy change
- Preparing talking points for a meeting agenda (not about specific cases)
- Brainstorming approaches to a program challenge
- Explaining a regulation in plain language

For green tasks: proceed. Use AI without modification. Focus on prompt quality.

**Yellow — Context-dependent, proceed with care**

Yellow tasks might involve sensitive information, but whether it's safe depends on how the task is framed and what information is included.

Examples:
- Drafting a case plan narrative (yellow if specific client details are included, green if it's a template)
- Preparing for a difficult conversation about an employee (yellow if it includes identifying details, green if it's a general planning exercise)
- Summarizing a report that contains aggregated data about clients (yellow if data could identify individuals)

For yellow tasks: ask clarifying questions. Can the task be accomplished without the sensitive information? Can you describe the situation in general terms rather than specific client terms? Can you use a hypothetical that captures the core challenge without identifying details?

**Red — High risk, do not proceed with AI**

Red tasks involve Personal Identifiable Information (PII), case numbers, client names, specific case details, protected health information, or other data that could identify an individual.

Examples:
- Any task that includes a client's name, date of birth, case number, or address
- Case notes or documentation about a specific individual
- Protected health information or mental health records
- Immigration status information
- Any document with client identifiers that can't be removed

For red tasks: stop. Do not proceed. Suggest alternative approaches that accomplish the work without the risky data. Often the underlying task can be accomplished with a hypothetical or by describing the general situation rather than the specific case.

## The Default Position

When in doubt, treat it as yellow and ask. It's better to pause and check than to proceed and create a privacy risk. The goal is not to block productive AI use — it's to make sure the use is appropriate for the specific context.

## Practical Guidance for HHS Managers

The most common mistake is including identifying details that aren't actually necessary for the task. Before you paste anything into an AI tool, ask: "Do I actually need this specific information in the prompt to get a good output?" Often the answer is no — you can describe the situation generally and get equally useful output.

The trust statement on AIforHHS.com reflects this principle: "AIforHHS never stores your conversations. Avoid entering client names, case numbers, or other identifying information."
