# Task Chain: Drafting an Email to Your Team

## Task Type
Draft

## Purpose
HHS managers write dozens of emails a week. Many of them are high-stakes: policy changes, difficult news, feedback on performance, calls to action, updates to leadership. Good emails in HHS settings require the right tone, the right level of detail, and clarity about what the reader is supposed to do. This task chain helps managers produce a strong first draft quickly, then refine it.

## Intake Question
"What's the email about, and who's the audience? Give me the key points and I'll draft it."

## Full Prompt Chain

**Role:** Act as a communications expert who specializes in clear, professional writing for public sector managers.

**Instruction:** Draft a professional email based on the following information. The email should be clear, direct, and appropriate for the relationship between sender and audience.

**Context:** [User provides: topic, audience (team/peers/leadership/clients/community), key points to include, any specific tone requirements]

**Constraints:** Keep it to [length as appropriate]. Use plain language. Lead with the most important information. Be clear about what action, if any, is needed from the reader. Avoid passive voice. No unnecessary qualifications or hedging.

**Output format:** Ready-to-send email with subject line, greeting, body, and closing.

## Power Follow-Up Options
- "Shorten this to 3 sentences"
- "Make the call to action clearer"
- "Rewrite this for a different audience" (e.g., from staff to leadership)
- "Make the tone warmer / more direct"
- "Add a bullet list of key points"

## Tone Guidance for HHS Contexts

**Communicating to frontline staff:** Direct, clear, supportive. Acknowledge the work. Be honest about challenges. Be specific about what you need.

**Communicating to leadership:** Concise, framed around impact and decision needs. Lead with the bottom line. Support with evidence. Make the ask explicit.

**Communicating about difficult news:** Lead with the fact, explain the context, clarify what this means for the recipient, offer support or next steps. Don't bury the difficult information.

**Communicating policy changes:** What's changing, when, why it matters, what they need to do differently, where to get help if they have questions.
